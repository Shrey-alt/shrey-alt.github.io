Project Overview

Fixing how India books
its hospital appointments.

Narayana Health is one of India's largest hospital chains — with 21 hospitals, 7,000+ beds, and patients across 55+ countries. Despite its scale and clinical excellence, its digital experience was fragmented, opaque, and losing patients at every step of the booking journey. We were brought in to reengineer the entire patient experience — from how they find a doctor to how they manage ongoing care — across four phases spanning 22+ months.

Problem Statement

World-class hospitals.
A broken digital front door.

Narayana Health's digital presence was scattered across multiple disconnected websites, apps, and portals — each with its own booking flow, payment logic, and user journey. Patients were abandoning bookings at every step. 50% never made it past the Find a Doctor screen. Those who did faced jargon-heavy specialty lists, no price transparency, and no way to book same-day appointments digitally. The system was reacting to patients instead of guiding them.

01
Fragmented Multi-Platform Experience
Multiple disconnected user websites, portals, and apps with no single patient identity. Each touchpoint felt like a different product — there was no continuity across the care journey.
02
Booking Abandonment at Scale
50% of users didn't proceed past the Find a Doctor screen. Most booking flows had 50% abandonment on Step 1 and 25% on Step 2. The funnel was haemorrhaging patients at every click.
03
Opaque Specialties & No Guidance
86% of usability test participants couldn't identify the right doctor specialty for their ailment. Medical jargon replaced intuitive guidance, leaving patients confused and resorting to the call centre.
04
Payment & Trust as Afterthoughts
Cost information was buried or absent. Same-day booking wasn't supported digitally — 55% of users said they'd call the centre for urgent appointments because the app couldn't handle it. No digital trust layer existed.

Research & Discovery

Listening to patients
before designing
for them.

We began by interviewing people who had recent experiences with doctors, hospitals, and clinics — mapping their emotional journey from first symptom to post-visit follow-up. We then followed that with data from Google Analytics to validate what we heard.

i.User interviews with recent healthcare visitors — mapping their full journey from symptom onset through appointment booking, consultation, and follow-up
ii.Empathy mapping — plotting the emotional highs and lows of the patient journey to identify moments of frustration, anxiety, and relief
iii.Google Analytics deep-dive — the top pages visited from homepage were /find-a-doctor, /specialties, and /hospitals, confirming that discovery and booking are the primary digital intent
iv.Usability testing sessions on live booking flows — revealing where and why patients were dropping off at each step of the appointment funnel
v.Focus groups with the Customer Excellence Team and "A Day in the Hospital" field research — speaking directly to patients and staff in the clinical environment

Guiding Principles

From reactive to
proactive patient experience.

Multiple disconnected user websites, portals & apps with no patient continuity
Single consumer identity & coherent user experience across all touchpoints
Multiple, disparate pathways to booking care with no unified tracking
Unified, trackable booking funnels optimised for conversion at every step
Payment as an afterthought — costs buried, no upfront pricing clarity
Transparent costs and simple payment methods shown early in the booking flow
Episodic interactions driven by volume-based metrics — patients treated transactionally
Improved patient retention, loyalty, and lifetime value-based care relationships
Reacting to consumer requests — no proactive guidance, prevention or wellness layer
Guiding next steps, prevention, and wellness — transitioning from hospital to health partner

UX Strategy

Trust.
Clarity.
Continuity.

01
Doctor Card Redesign — Trust at First Glance
Post-testing, doctor cards were rebuilt around the signals users actually need: years of practice, photo, clear specialty name in plain language, languages spoken, and upfront consultation price. All five were absent or buried in the original design.
02
Symptom-First Discovery, Specialty-Second
Instead of asking patients to self-identify a medical specialty — which 86% couldn't do — the new flow leads with a symptom or condition search. Specialty mapping happens invisibly, behind an intelligent routing layer that guides patients to the right doctor without medical jargon.
03
Transparent Cost Architecture
Consultation fees, procedure estimates, and insurance compatibility were surfaced at the point of decision — not hidden until payment. Removing the pricing surprise at the end of a booking flow dramatically improved funnel completion.
04
Same-Day Digital Booking
Designing a same-day slot surfacing mechanism directly answered the 55% of users who resorted to the call centre for urgent bookings. Real-time slot availability with clear time proximity labels ("Available now", "2 slots today") gave digital confidence to a previously phone-only behaviour.
05
Unified Patient Identity & Health Score
Phase 2 introduced a Patient Healthcare Portal with a single patient identity across all touchpoints. Phase 2.1 added an innovative Health Score — a personalised wellness metric that rewards patients for healthy exercise and diet behaviours, transforming Narayana from a treatment provider into a long-term health partner.

Outcome & Learnings

Healthcare UX is
trust design
at every step.

The most important lesson from this project was that every element of the healthcare booking experience is a trust signal — not just the doctor card, but the price, the progress indicator, the confirmation email, and the language of the UI. Patients are vulnerable at the point of booking care. Every design decision either builds or erodes their confidence in the system.

◈
Jargon is abandonment
When 86% of users can't choose a doctor specialty, the problem isn't the user — it's the language. Replacing medical taxonomy with symptom-led discovery was the single most impactful UX change in Phase 1.
◈
Photos and language spoken are non-negotiable trust signals
Usability testing revealed that users caring for elderly parents specifically needed to know if the doctor spoke their parent's language. This insight — surfaced only through qualitative research — became a required field in the doctor card.
◈
Same-day booking is a digital retention problem
55% of patients calling the centre for urgent appointments represents not just a UX failure but a retention risk — each call is a patient who might book elsewhere. Bridging this gap digitally was as much a business case as a design one.
◈
Health Score transforms the patient relationship
Phase 2.1's Health Score concept — rewarding patients for diet and exercise — represents the biggest strategic shift: from hospital that treats illness to health partner that prevents it. This changes the lifetime value equation and the emotional relationship between patient and provider.
