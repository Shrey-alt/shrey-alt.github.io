Project Overview

Keeping drivers safe
on every kilometre
of every shift.

Professional drivers — from long-haul truckers to last-mile delivery riders — operate in conditions where fatigue, distraction, and poor habits cost lives. Google Smart Drive is a mobile-first driver activity tracking app that monitors behaviour in real time, scores each trip, flags fatigue before it becomes dangerous, and gives fleet managers a live intelligence layer across their entire operation.

Problem Statement

Drivers are monitored by
hardware, not helped by intelligence.

The fleet management industry generates billions in revenue tracking vehicles — GPS coordinates, mileage, fuel consumption. But the driver, the most critical variable in road safety, is an afterthought. Fatigue kills. Distraction kills. Yet most fleet apps are dashboards built for operations managers, not tools built for the human behind the wheel. Google Smart Drive set out to flip that equation entirely.

01
Fatigue Goes Undetected Until It's Too Late
17.6% of all fatal crashes involve a drowsy driver — but most fleet apps have no fatigue detection layer. Drivers routinely exceed safe hours without any intelligent alert.
02
Driver Behaviour Data Serves Managers, Not Drivers
Existing telematics tools funnel data to fleet managers as scorecards. Drivers never see their own data in real time — removing the feedback loop that actually changes behaviour.
03
Fragmented Tools Across Trip, Rest & Compliance
Drivers juggle separate apps for routing, ELD logging, rest timers, and incident reporting. Cognitive overload from switching tools is itself a distraction hazard.
04
No Positive Reinforcement Mechanism
Fleet apps punish poor driving after the fact with demerits and incident flags. There is no system that actively rewards safe habits, creates momentum, or builds driver confidence.

Research & Discovery

Following the driver,
not the fleet manager's
spreadsheet.

We embedded with drivers across three segments — long-haul freight, urban last-mile delivery, and ride-share — conducting shadow research across real shifts to understand the lived experience of a professional driver.

i.Shadow research riding along on 34 driver shifts across freight, delivery, and ride-share segments — logging micro-frustrations, fatigue signs, and tool-switching patterns in real time
ii.In-depth interviews with 28 professional drivers and 12 fleet managers — mapping the tension between operational efficiency demands and individual driver safety needs
iii.Analysis of FMCSA Hours-of-Service compliance data and NHTSA fatigue crash statistics — grounding design decisions in the regulatory and safety realities of professional driving
iv.Competitive teardown of 9 fleet apps (Samsara, KeepTruckin, Geotab, Lytx, etc.) — identifying the consistent gap: every product is built for the dashboard, not the driver

UX Strategy

Built for
the driver.
Not the
report.

01
Driver-First Information Architecture
Every screen hierarchy is built around what a driver needs in the next 30 seconds — not what a manager wants in a weekly report. Critical safety info lives one tap from anywhere.
02
Real-Time Fatigue Intelligence
Continuous drive-time tracking with progressive fatigue alerts at 4h, 6h and 7.5h thresholds — aligned with FMCSA HOS rules. AI-driven rest-stop recommendations surface before impairment sets in.
03
Transparent, Gamified Driver Scoring
Per-trip safety scores broken down by acceleration, braking, cornering, speed, and phone usage — visible to the driver in real time. Streaks, badges, and leaderboards turn safety into motivation.
04
Unified Activity Hub
Trip log, HOS timer, rest alerts, route history, and incident reports consolidated into a single app — eliminating the multi-tool context-switching that itself creates distraction risk.
05
Fleet Intelligence Layer
Managers get a live mobile dashboard with driver-level safety scores, fatigue flags, route deviations, and compliance status — accessible from anywhere, formatted for a 6-inch screen.

Outcome & Learnings

Safety UX is
not a warning,
it's a relationship.

The most important design insight from this project was that safety alerts work only when drivers trust the system that sends them. We spent as much time designing the tone and timing of nudges as we did the screens themselves — because a fatigue alert ignored is worse than no alert at all. Trust was earned by being accurate, contextual, and never crying wolf.

◈
Drivers want feedback, not surveillance
When drivers owned their own score data — instead of it going straight to their manager — engagement tripled. Transparency and control over personal data was the single biggest trust driver.
◈
Fatigue UX must be graduated, not binary
A single "you're fatigued" alert is ignored. Progressive nudges at 4h, 6h, and 7.5h — each with a different tone, urgency, and suggested action — created a 4× higher action rate vs a single warning.
◈
Gamification drives 2× more improvement than penalties
Drivers who received positive score streaks and badges improved 2× faster than those who only received incident flags. The reward loop turned safety into a game worth winning.
◈
Fleet managers need exceptions, not averages
The fleet dashboard redesign taught us that managers don't need to see 24 driver scores — they need to immediately spot the 2 that are dangerous. Exception-first design saved critical response time.

AI-Powered Predictive Coaching
Move from reactive fatigue alerts to predictive coaching — using drive pattern ML to flag risk before the 6-hour mark hits, based on each driver's personal performance baseline.

Wearable Integration Layer
